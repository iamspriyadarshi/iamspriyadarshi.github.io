What do we need to create something? The answer is a bit of information, some raw material, and energy. Let's understand this with an example of a 3D printer. A 3D printer is a machine that can make anything of any shape which was not there before. Here, in the case of a 3D printer, information is provided by the computer in the form of bits which is created by us in the form of the design of that object, the raw material is plastic, and energy is in the form of electricity.  
  
The mother is analogous to 3D printer. She reproduces a human being. In this case, she takes information that is between both mother and father in the form of genetic code, some cellular raw material, and energy in the form of Adenosine triphosphate (ATP). After several months, she reproduces a human being that was not there in existence before.  
  
The question that arises here is that how much information does it take to build and assemble humans? How much space is required to keep that information? Is it very little? Is it too much? The building block of human body is the cell. The human being is a cluster of cells, so if we assemble the right cells at the right place, then we can build a human. We know that different parts of the body have different organizations with different types of cells. The genetic code contains all the information about what is the organization of cells at certain body parts. If we try to save the information required to build a baby in a thumb drive, it will fill the entire titanic of thumb drive multiplied 3000 times or even more. This is the miracle of life. A pregnant lady is assembling the biggest amount of information that exists. Nature is very creative and smarter than humans. In just 4 billion years, nature managed to pack that much information in the chromosome.

Now to build human beings, we first need to read the code of life i.e., genetic code. Rosalind Franklin took a rough picture of it for the first time. From the time of her discovery, it took more than 40 years to poke inside the cell, take out the chromosome, unpack it to take a string of Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA), and read the code. This code comes out to be just 4 letters: A for  _Adenine_, T for  _Thymine_, C for  _Cytosine_, and G for  _Guanine_. The genetic code is a combination of these 4 letters. This is analogous to Claude Shannon's  _Information Theory_ which says that any piece of digital information can be represented with 0s and 1s.

The sequencing of the Human Genome was done by the Human Genome Project and genetic code was stored in typed form in books. The number of books required just to store the code of human DNA  comes out to be 3300, and each book contains 1000 pages, and each page contains 1000 letters. If you miss a letter from it, you will get a genetic disease. You can see the perfection of nature from this. The exciting thing here is that only 10 million letters i.e., 10000 pages of these books make differences in humans. Rest of the codes are identical in all the humans. These ten million letters are responsible for human diversity like difference in heights, complexions, etc.  

The most challenging part is to read that genetic code. We have to find out which human characteristics are produced by genetic code combination. We all know the human mind cannot find this out. This requires heavy computational power. We can try to solve this problem by using various computer science methods like gene sequencing, statistical models, machine learning, etc. We first have to collect the biggest database of humans including its phenotypes and every type of human detail possible. Next steps would be to collect human genomes. So, we have to build a machine and teach it to understand and translate the genome into phenotype. We have to find out what are those letters and what they mean? I think that it is not going to be simple. It is going to be most complicated for those ten million letters which make differences in humans. We need to start with common traits as they are the same in all humans. Many scientists are working on it. They have even managed to sequence certain phenotypes.  
  
You might be thinking why we need to do all this? Suppose if we manage to know the genomic code responsible for height. Then, it will be possible to make people taller or shorter by modifying the code using CRISPR technology. We would know how our body works, how the disease develops, and how medicine works. If we managed to find how the disease develops and how medicine works, then there will be a new revolution in the medical industry called personalized medicine.
